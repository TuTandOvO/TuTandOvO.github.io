Omics of me — sequencing my twin and me

From a childhood photo to a whole-genome answer — the reasoning and the data flow behind asking, honestly, whether DNA explains why I've been the heavier fraternal twin.

A personal-genomics side project — and an exercise in honest reasoning. n = 2, exploratory, hypothesis-generating. No causal claim is made or implied.

It started with a photo

My fraternal twin brother and me as kids — that's me on the left. Same womb, same home, same dinner table, and yet I've been the rounder one for as long as there are photos to prove it.

For most of my life, "why am I the bigger one?" was just a family joke. Then I stepped into bioinformatics at the ZJU–UoE Joint Institute (ZJE) — and realised I could stop guessing and actually ask the data.

×

So I followed the question all the way down — from a tube of blood to 3 billion base-pairs, reasoning through every step. Here is that flow.

1
From blood to reads

Two brothers, two genomes

I reached out to BGI, gave a tube of whole blood, and had both of our genomes sequenced at ~60× depth on the DNBSEQ short-read platform. Sequencing can't read a chromosome end-to-end, so the DNA is shattered into hundreds of millions of short fragments (read 1, read 2, read 3 …), each ~150 letters long, and every position is covered ~60 times so we can tell a true variant from a sequencing slip.

→ → ~150 bp reads · ~60×

2
Why a pangenome, not the standard reference

The standard genome isn't built for me

To make sense of reads you align them to a reference genome. The textbook choice, GRCh38 (hg38), is built largely from European-ancestry DNA. Aligning my East-Asian genome to a European-leaning reference quietly loses accuracy exactly where I differ from it. The fix is a pangenome: a graph woven from many ancestries at once, so my reads can follow a path that actually fits me.

3
Mapping with vg Giraffe

How reads find their place in a graph

A graph reference has many branching paths, so mapping isn't a simple line-up. vg counts my k-mers to pull out a personalized graph (only the haplotypes that look like me), then Giraffe threads each read through that graph, and finally surjects the result back onto GRCh38 coordinates as a standard BAM file the rest of the world can read.

The vg / Giraffe pangenome mapping workflow: reads → personalized graph → graph alignment → surject to a linear BAM.
4
Variant calling with Clair3

Finding where my letters changed

With the BAM and the reference, the next job is to read out, position by position, where I differ from the reference. Clair3 does this with two deep-learning models working together:

Pileup model — fast first pass. The column of bases stacked over each position feeds a small network; most sites are settled instantly.
Full-alignment model — for the hard sites. Near indels and repeats, the entire 2-D local alignment is re-read by a deeper network.

Clair3 is the lightweight, GPU-free stand-in for DeepVariant; it also catches structural variants via vg call. Out comes a per-person genotype at every position: 0/0 reference, 0/1 heterozygous, 1/1 homozygous-variant.

6
Annotation & obesity genetics

Of the millions of differences, which could matter for weight?

Ensembl VEP annotates every variant — which gene, what consequence, how rare, predicted damage — and I keep only sites where the two of us truly differ at high confidence (both GQ ≥ 20, DP ≥ 10). Then I ask the obesity question three ways, each grounded in the literature rather than picked by eye:

Why look at FTO, MC4R, GNPDA2… at all? Because these are the loci the field has repeatedly tied to body weight. The reasoning rests on:

7
The answer

An honest negative

Three independent readings all point the same way: zero monogenic obesity-gene variants differ between us; our genome-wide BMI polygenic scores are essentially identical (within noise, and nominally higher in my leaner brother); and the one classic BMI variant that does differ, GNPDA2, carries more risk in him, not me.

So — does DNA explain it?

On this data, no. Common-variant genetics does not account for why I've been the heavier twin, and if anything leans the other way. A lifelong difference between two people who shared a womb, a home and a diet is most likely driven by something genetics can't see here — early-life environment, behaviour and epigenetics. That isn't the dramatic answer, but it's the honest one, and reaching it cleanly was the entire point.
